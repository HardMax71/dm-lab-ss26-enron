\begin{frame}{What Enron was}
  Enron was a large US energy and commodities trading company based in
  Houston. After a decade of rapid growth it collapsed in late 2001 in
  an accounting-fraud scandal, then among the largest US bankruptcies.

  \medskip
  The mailboxes survived because the Federal Energy Regulatory
  Commission placed them in the public record during the investigation.
  Carnegie Mellon hosts the cleaned version we use.

  \medskip
  Dataset:
  \href{https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~enron/}{\texttt{cs.cmu.edu/\textasciitilde enron/}}
  \hfill\small\texttt{enron\_mail\_20150507.tar.gz}
\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{What we have}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item About \textbf{517{,}000} email files
    \item From \textbf{150} mailbox owners
    \item Covering \textbf{1997 to 2002} (Enron's active years)
    \item Unpacked: roughly \textbf{2.6\,GB} of plain text
  \end{itemize}

  \medskip
  \small
  A few hundred files carry broken dates and a handful of folders mix
  sent and received mail, so later plots are drawn after a light
  cleaning pass.
\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}[fragile]{One file, one email}
  Every message is a plain-text file in the RFC-822 format. A block of
  headers is followed by the body.

  \medskip
  \scriptsize
  \begin{verbatim}
Message-ID: <1326004.1075846655910.JavaMail.evans@thyme>
Date:       Wed, 18 Apr 2001 04:08:00 -0700 (PDT)
From:       mary.cook@enron.com
To:         angela.davis@enron.com, ... , tana.jones@enron.com
Subject:    Guaranty Distribution
X-From:     Mary Cook
X-Origin:   BAILEY-S
X-Folder:   \Susan_Bailey_June2001\Notes Folders\All documents

As a reminder, paralegals should distribute guaranties as stated ...
  \end{verbatim}
\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Variables}
  The fields below are kept from each message. \texttt{X-*} fields are
  Outlook-specific and tell us which mailbox the file came from.

  \medskip
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lll}
    \toprule
    Field        & Example value                & Data type \\
    \midrule
    Message-ID   & \texttt{<1326004...@thyme>}  & Nominal (unique string) \\
    Date         & 18 Apr 2001 04:08 PDT        & Datetime \\
    From         & \texttt{mary.cook@enron.com} & Nominal (categorical) \\
    To           & list of addresses            & Nominal (list) \\
    Subject      & Guaranty Distribution        & Text \\
    X-From       & Mary Cook                    & Text (display name) \\
    X-Origin     & BAILEY-S                     & Mailbox identifier \\
    Message body & free text                    & Text (freeform) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Open challenges}
  \small
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{91.9\%} of subjects are shared across multiple
          emails, weakening subject-based threading
    \item Body text needs cleaning before any NLP (forwarded blocks,
          attachment placeholders, quoted replies)
    \item Many files are duplicated across sender and recipient
          mailboxes, plus extra copies from forwarding
    \item Engineered header features are mostly uncorrelated so far
    \item Surfacing notable individual messages is still manual
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{frame}{Where we stand}
  The corpus is large, real, and unevenly distributed. Volume, folder
  shape, sender domains, activity rhythm, and vocabulary all line up
  with what we would expect from a real US energy trader.

  \medskip
  The clearest single finding is the deletion spike in late 2001 to
  mid-2002, which sits exactly on top of Enron's public collapse.
  Thread reconstruction is the largest open problem and the focus for
  next week.

\end{frame}
